## Voice and tone

Write like a clear technical operator, not like a pitch deck. Short sentences are fine. Strong opinions are fine. Vague claims are not.

The tone target:

- Precise enough for engineers and decision makers.
- Human enough to stay readable in public.
- Confident enough to avoid hedging on basic facts.

## Formatting rules

Use Markdown as if it is the primary medium, not a fallback export format.

| Do | Do not |
| --- | --- |
| Use descriptive headings | Use headings that only sound impressive |
| Show examples and command lines | Hide the mechanics behind abstractions |
| Link to adjacent pages | Strand the reader on isolated pages |
| Keep paragraphs compact | Publish large walls of text |

## Link style

Internal links should move readers forward through the handbook. They should not feel like dead-end references.

For example:

- Link from structure to workflow.
- Link from workflow to standards.
- Link from design language to the brand-conscious pages on the site.

## Public writing checklist

Before publishing, confirm the page:

1. Has a concrete claim in the first screenful.
2. Explains why the topic matters to an external reader.
3. Uses examples, rules, or evidence instead of soft positioning language.
4. Ends with a useful next step or related page.

> Public writing becomes stronger when each page can stand alone and still connect cleanly to the rest of the handbook.
